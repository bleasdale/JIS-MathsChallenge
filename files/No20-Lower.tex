      \begin{MySolutionBox}
        Since we can trust Portia, on each lid, both statements can be true; one can be true and the other false; both statements cannot be false.\par
        We proceed by cases: we assume the portrait is in each casket in turn. We eliminate the cases that give rise to impossible situations.\par
        \underline{Case 1: The portrait is in the gold casket}\par
        \hspace{1em}Gold: \;Statement 1 is false: Statement 2 is either true or false.\par
        \hspace{1em}Silver: Statement 1 is false: Statement 2 is either false or true.\par
        Of the two Statement 2's on the gold and silver boxes, at least one must be false which means that on one of these boxes there are two false statements, contradicting Portia. \par\medskip
        \underline{Case 2: The portrait is in the silver casket}\par
        \hspace{1em}Gold: \;Statement 1 is true: Statement 2 is either true or false.\par
        \hspace{1em}Silver: Statement 1 is true: Statement 2 is either false or true.\par
        \hspace{1em}Lead: \;Statement 1 is true: Statement 2 is true.\par
        We have no inconsistency.\par\medskip
        \underline{Case 3: The portrait is in the lead casket}\par
        \hspace{1em}Gold: \;Statement 1 is false: Statement 2 is false.\par
        Again, our assumption has led to a contradiction.\par\medskip
        Case 2 did not arrive at a logical impossibility, so, the portrait is in the silver casket.\par\medskip
